  • What is Congressional Debate?

    Congressional Debate is a nationally recognized form of debate that emulates the United States Congress. Rather than debate against one person or on teams, individuals instead present speeches and cross examine competitors in a room of 16-24 people to support or refute hypothetical legislation.

    In Congress there will often be a preset list of topics. Students will write legislation on what to do about one of the topics. All Congress competitors will also prepare three-minute speeches on the legislation chosen for debate, debating the pros and cons of passing that legislation. It is not necessary to have a speech on every topic, and you get to choose your own side. After speeches, there is a cross examination of the speaker by the entire room of competitors.

    Debate rotates from topic to topic with speakers either supporting or refuting the chosen legislation. Whether the actual legislation gets passed is of no importance. Competitors are judged on the quality of their speeches and cross examination of others.

    Judges prepare ballots with feedback for the competitors which are returned to the competitors after the competition with judge comments and scores.

  • How is Congressional Debate different than other types of debates?

    There are four types of debate; Lincoln Douglas, Public Forum, Policy, and Congressional Debate. All four types of debate are recognized on the local, state and national level. Congressional Debate's basic premise is similar to Model UN, however the judging, procedure, and nature of debate is entirely different.

    Topics:
    In Lincoln-Douglas debate, resolutions (topics to be debated) change every two months. In Public Forum Debate, resolutions usually change every month. Lastly, in Policy, teams will debate one topic for the whole year.

    Congressional debate is especially unique when it comes to how many topics members debate. At each local tournament, you will debate 3 domestic topics, 3 economic topics, and 3 foreign topics. At national tournaments, you will debate up to 12 topics in preliminary rounds, 6 in out-rounds, and 3 in finals.

    The types of topics debated also differ. In Public-Forum and Policy Debate, you will often debate political topics. In Lincoln-Douglas, topics are more centered on morals and ethics. Congress topics center on a combination of political and ethical issues. Examples include:

    -Should we accept Syrian Refugees?
    -Should we sanction Russia?
    -Should we pull out troops of Afghanistan?
    -Should we institute a 15% flat tax for corporations?
    -Should we make Community College Free?
    -Should we ban Affirmative Action?
    -Should we make Vaccines Mandatory?

    Procedures:
    Public Forum Debate and Policy Debate are 2 vs. 2, while Lincoln-Douglas Debate is 1 vs. 1. In Congressional Debate, however, you will be in a room of around 20 competitors, debating individually for yourself. Your goal is to persuade the room to vote in your side of the legislation with eloquently phrased speeches and a witty cross-examination performance.

    While all other debate types focus primarily on the content of their arguments or giving as many arguments as possible in a short amount of time, Congress is unique in that although argumentation is important, the delivery of arguments counts for a lot as well. Unlike other debates, a successful Congressional Debater will be able to appeal to one's humanity and persuade them, by utilizing meaningful rhetoric in addition to their content analysis.

  • What awards does the TJ Congressional Debate Team hold?

    TJ's Congressional Debate Team holds the most national and local awards of all TJ debate teams. In it's 5 years of existence, the team has become the largest-standing debate team at TJ with the most amount of TOC (Tournament of Champions) bids.

    Apart from TJ, our team has become the largest and most successful debate team in Virginia. In fact, we have been recognized by the National Speech and Debate Association for being a top-20 national debate team.
